# [Introduction to Human-Robot Interaction: A Multi-Perspective   Introductory Course](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.15323)

## Write a very high-quality and detailed summary of the paper that describes the paper in a way that a human can fully understand. The summary should cover the problem, the proposed solution and highlight the main contributions of the paper.

 Here is a detailed summary of the paper:

This paper describes the design of an introductory course in Human-Robot Interaction (HRI) intended for undergraduate and graduate engineering students. The goal of the course is three-fold: (1) introduce students to the concept of social robotics and using robots to meet human needs, (2) provide an overview of the HRI field and research methods used, and (3) teach qualitative, design, computational, and quantitative research methods that can be applied in other engineering contexts. 

The course is offered at a small engineering university with no social science or humanities programs. As a result, it targets students in computer science, mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, and robotics who likely have strong technical skills but little exposure to relevant theories and methods from psychology, philosophy, etc. The course format includes lectures, lab assignments with Nao robots, and a major semester-long group project.

The syllabus walks through the entire design-implement-evaluate cycle. It starts with needfinding via stakeholder interviews, moves to identifying design goals grounded in those needs, then covers interaction design using storyboarding and embodied sketching. Next it focuses on technical implementation skills with the Nao, followed by quantitative evaluation via an HRI experiment created and run by the students. The semester ends with analysis of results and reflections on the overall process.

Deliverables include reading quizzes, lab reports, presentations, an autonomous robot interaction video, and a paper modeled after HRI publications. The group project ties together the qualitative, design, technical, and quantitative components in one complete example following real HRI research methodology.

In summary, this is a project-driven, methods-focused HRI course intended to give engineering students, especially those in robotics-related majors, exposure to HRI concepts and techniques they are unlikely to see otherwise. It aims to ground technical work in genuine human needs and teach a wide skillset spanning social science to robot programming.
