Here are a few thoughts on how this paper compares to other research in lane detection:- The paper proposes a new anchor decomposition approach to make anchor-based lane detection more flexible and adaptable. Most prior anchor-based methods assume lanes originate from the image edges, which limits their applicability. By generating start points and directions as heatmaps, the anchors are not constrained to image edges. This is a notable improvement over previous anchor-based methods.- For capturing lane shape context, the paper introduces a Large Kernel Attention module integrated with FPN. Using larger convolutional kernels to aggregate features is an interesting idea for this task, as lanes span the entire image vertically. This is a distinct approach from other methods that tend to rely on smaller kernels.- The proposed General Lane IoU loss seems to better handle cases where the prediction and ground truth lanes do not align well, compared to the standard LIoU loss. Evaluating on VIL-100 shows GLIoU helps boost performance over LIoU.- Overall accuracy is very competitive with state-of-the-art methods on major datasets like CULane, TuSimple, and especially VIL-100 where it achieves a new top result. The decomposition approach appears to make the anchor-based framework more robust.- The method still relies on anchor regression, which has some limitations compared to segmenting lanes directly or predicting polynomial coefficients. But the flexibility added by the proposed decomposition is a noteworthy improvement over past anchor-based techniques.In summary, the anchor decomposition idea and integration of larger convolution kernels seem to be the major novelties of this paper compared to prior lane detection research. The gains on VIL-100 highlight the advantages of the more flexible anchor representation. It's an interesting direction to make anchor-based approaches more generalized.
