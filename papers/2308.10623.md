# [GaitPT: Skeletons Are All You Need For Gait Recognition](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.10623)

## What is the central research question or hypothesis that this paper addresses?

The main research goal of this paper is to propose a new skeleton-based architecture for gait recognition that improves upon previous state-of-the-art methods. Specifically, the authors introduce GaitPT, a hierarchical transformer model that leverages spatial and temporal attention mechanisms to capture both local and global gait patterns from skeleton sequences. The key hypothesis is that modelling the natural hierarchical structure and motion of the human body will lead to better gait recognition performance compared to prior approaches like graph neural networks or flat transformer models. The experiments on three benchmark datasets (CASIA-B, GREW, Gait3D) validate this hypothesis, with GaitPT achieving new state-of-the-art results.In summary, the central research question is: Can a hierarchical transformer architecture designed with anatomical priors improve skeleton-based gait recognition accuracy over previous methods? The authors empirically demonstrate the superiority of their proposed GaitPT model in answering this question.


## What is the main contribution of this paper?

The main contribution of this paper is proposing GaitPT, a novel skeleton-based gait recognition architecture that achieves state-of-the-art performance. The key ideas are:- GaitPT adopts a hierarchical transformer architecture to effectively capture both spatial and temporal features of gait in a structure guided by the human skeleton. - It processes gait information in an anatomically consistent manner, first modeling joint-level motion, then limb-level, limb group-level, and finally full body motion. This allows capturing both micro and macro gait patterns.- GaitPT is evaluated on three popular gait recognition benchmarks - CASIA-B, GREW, and Gait3D. It obtains state-of-the-art results compared to previous skeleton-based methods, with over 6% average accuracy increase on CASIA-B and around 6% rank-1 accuracy increase on GREW and Gait3D.- Ablation studies demonstrate the importance of the hierarchical design and the effectiveness of spatial and temporal attention for modeling gait. The results also show a high correlation between pose estimator performance and downstream gait recognition accuracy.In summary, the main contribution is proposing a novel anatomically-inspired transformer architecture for skeleton-based gait recognition that achieves new state-of-the-art results on major benchmarks and advances the capability for gait modeling. The hierarchical design and spatial-temporal attention are key to the improved performance.

## How does this paper compare to other research in the same field?

Here are my thoughts on how this paper compares to other research in the field of gait recognition:- This paper presents a novel deep learning architecture called GaitPT for skeleton-based gait recognition. It builds on prior work using graph neural networks for modeling skeleton sequences, as well as recent advances in vision transformers. - Compared to previous graph-based methods like GaitGraph and JointsGait, GaitPT achieves significantly higher accuracy on standard gait recognition benchmarks like CASIA-B, improving the state-of-the-art by over 6%. This suggests the transformer architecture is better able to model spatial and temporal features of gait from skeletons.- GaitPT also outperforms the only other transformer-based method GaitFormer by a large margin on CASIA-B. This highlights the importance of the hierarchical design and spatial attention in GaitPT, compared to just temporal attention in GaitFormer.- For in-the-wild gait recognition, GaitPT achieves state-of-the-art results on GREW and Gait3D compared to previous skeleton-based methods, and even outperforms some silhouette-based techniques. This demonstrates its strong capability for generalizing to unconstrained real-world scenarios.- The paper provides useful ablation studies and analysis on the contribution of each component of GaitPT. It also highlights the impact of pose estimation performance on downstream gait recognition accuracy.- One limitation is that GaitPT is only evaluated on datasets mostly containing Asian subjects. Additional diversity in training data could help the model generalize better.Overall, the hierarchical transformer design of GaitPT clearly advances the state-of-the-art in skeleton-based gait recognition over prior graph-based and transformer models. The strong performance on both controlled and in-the-wild benchmarks demonstrates its capabilities for deployments requiring gait identification. More work on diverse data and real-world validation would further strengthen its usefulness.


## What future research directions do the authors suggest?

Based on my reading of the paper, here are some of the main future research directions suggested by the authors:- Evaluating the model on more diverse and challenging datasets to assess generalization capabilities. The authors note that the datasets used (CASIA-B, GREW, Gait3D) only cover a subset of real-world walking variations. Testing on more varied data could reveal limitations.- Exploring different partitioning schemes for the intermediate layers of the hierarchical model. The authors tested a few options for combining joint embeddings in Stage 3 but suggest there may be better ways to model limb interactions. - Applying the hierarchical transformer approach to other pose-based tasks like action recognition or gesture analysis. The authors propose the architecture may be useful for other skeleton-based problems beyond just gait.- Pretraining pose estimation models on more varied data to improve skeleton extraction quality. The authors show pose estimation performance greatly impacts gait recognition accuracy, so advances here could boost results.- Combining skeleton-based modeling with some appearance information for better performance. The paper focuses on a pure skeleton-based approach but fusing in some silhouette or image data may help.- Testing new self-supervised objectives for representation learning. The model uses a triplet loss but contrastive, predictive, or other losses may lead to better features.- Adapting the model for pathological gait analysis applications in healthcare. The authors mention gait analysis for detecting abnormalities as a beneficial application area to explore.In summary, the main suggestions are to evaluate the current approach more thoroughly, explore architectural variants, apply the model to new domains, improve the input data quality, and investigate alternative training schemes or applications. Advancing pose estimation and using more varied datasets seem like critical next steps.
